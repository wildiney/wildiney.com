When I started in design, tools did not deliver effects ready-made. Creating a realistic shadow required understanding how light actually works: direction, temperature, and a bit of the inverse square law. It meant multiple layers in Photoshop, each handling a different aspect of light behavior. For animation, it was the same: the principles of squash and stretch came from the physics of objects in motion, not from a prompt. Physics was a required first-year course in my degree for a reason.

You had to understand in order to do.

AI automated and compressed that layer of understanding so we can reach polished layouts without knowing how they work. Productivity moved to the center, and that makes sense. It is not a reversible path and there is nothing useful in being nostalgic about it. The move now is to occupy the space AI has not reached yet: the connections it has not mapped, the problems it does not know exist.

You are no longer the mechanic. You are the driver who knows mechanics. I use that analogy because driving AI is like driving a vehicle: you only feel confident when you feel the tires gripping the road, the car under control. The worst feeling is when you lose traction for a second, like hydroplaning over a sheet of water. If you are experienced, you know the environment, you know your tools, and the damage is controlled or avoided. For someone without that experience, the obvious move is to hit the brakes.

Would that be your choice too?
